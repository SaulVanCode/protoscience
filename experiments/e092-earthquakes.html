01 — What is this data?

The USGS Earthquake Hazards Program continuously monitors seismic activity worldwide. We pulled the 30-day catalog of all earthquakes magnitude 2.5 and above — 1,930 events with measured magnitudes, depths, locations, and timestamps.

We gave ProtoScience the raw seismic catalog. No mention of Gutenberg-Richter. No seismology textbook. Just earthquake records.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Gutenberg-Richter Law

log10(N≥M) = 5.78 − 0.81·M with R²=0.915 and b=0.81. The expected b-value is ~1.0. The discovered value falls within the normal range for tectonic regions.

Depth Distribution

81% shallow (<70km), 15% intermediate (70-300km), 4% deep (>300km). The dominance of shallow events reflects crustal fault mechanics.

Poisson Process

Inter-event time CV=1.095 (a perfect Poisson process has CV=1.0). Earthquake occurrence is statistically indistinguishable from a random process at the global scale.

Magnitude-Depth Independence

Weak correlation r=0.26 between magnitude and depth. Large earthquakes can occur at any depth — magnitude is not controlled by how deep the fault ruptures.

04 — Why it matters

The Gutenberg-Richter law is seismology's most fundamental empirical relationship: for every magnitude step, earthquakes become roughly 10 times less frequent. It was established in 1944 through decades of careful observation. ProtoScience recovered it from a single month of USGS data.

The Poisson-process confirmation and depth distribution both emerged without prompting, demonstrating that the system can discover not just power laws but also statistical properties of natural processes. These results validate ProtoScience's ability to work with geophysical data — a domain far removed from astronomy where it has been most tested.
