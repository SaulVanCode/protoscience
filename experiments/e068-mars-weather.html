01 — What is this data?

NASA's Curiosity rover has been measuring the Martian atmosphere daily since landing in Gale Crater in August 2012. The REMS (Rover Environmental Monitoring Station) instrument records temperature, pressure, humidity, UV radiation, and wind every sol (Martian day). This dataset spans 4,583 sols — over 7 Mars years — making it the longest continuous weather record from another planet.

We gave ProtoScience the raw climate measurements with solar longitude (Ls, the Martian "calendar") as the time coordinate. No atmospheric chemistry. No orbital mechanics. Just daily weather readings from Mars.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Sinusoidal temperature

T = -42.9 + 9.3·sin(2π·Ls/360 + 0.33), R²=0.779. A clean sine wave with a 20°C seasonal amplitude, centered at -43°C.

22% pressure variation

Atmospheric pressure swings from 715 to 896 Pa — a 22% variation. On Earth, seasonal pressure changes are less than 1%. On Mars, CO₂ literally freezes out of the atmosphere at the poles.

CO₂ freeze-thaw cycle

The pressure minimum coincides with southern winter: CO₂ snows onto the south polar cap, removing ~25% of the atmosphere. When spring arrives, it sublimates back.

Steady-state system

SINDy found no significant differential coupling between variables. Mars weather is a steady-state seasonal oscillation, not a dynamical system like Earth's chaotic climate.

05 — Why this matters

Mars is the only other planet where we have ground-truth daily weather data spanning multiple years. The 22% pressure cycle is extraordinary — it means Mars seasonally loses and regains a quarter of its atmosphere. No other planet in the solar system does this.

For future Mars missions and eventual colonization, understanding the pressure cycle is critical: it affects landing aerodynamics (thinner atmosphere = faster descent), life support (CO₂ availability for oxygen production), and even rocket fuel manufacturing (Sabatier reaction depends on ambient CO₂ pressure).
