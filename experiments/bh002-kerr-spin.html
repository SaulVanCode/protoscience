01 — What is this data?

Real black holes spin. The Kerr metric describes the geometry of spacetime around a rotating black hole, parameterized by mass M and dimensionless spin a/M (0 = no spin, 1 = maximum spin). We fixed M=10 and swept spin from 0 to 0.998 across 40 values.

ProtoScience received only the numerical output — ISCO radii, shadow shapes, binding energies, and frame-dragging rates — with no knowledge of general relativity or the Kerr solution.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

ISCO Collapse

The innermost stable orbit shrinks from 6M (no spin) to 1.24M (near-extremal spin) — a 5-fold collapse. Matter can orbit much closer to a spinning black hole.

Binding Energy Surge

Radiative efficiency jumps from 5.7% to 32.1% as spin increases. A maximally spinning black hole converts nearly a third of accreted mass into energy — far beyond nuclear fusion's 0.7%.

D-Shaped Shadow

Shadow asymmetry grows from 0 to 0.124. Frame-dragging flattens one side of the shadow, producing the characteristic D-shape seen in EHT simulations.

Frame Dragging

Frame-dragging angular velocity emerges from zero and spikes near extremal spin. Space itself is dragged along with the rotating black hole — the Lense-Thirring effect.

04 — Why it matters

The Kerr solution is one of the most important exact solutions in all of physics. It predicts that a spinning black hole drags spacetime with it, allowing matter to orbit closer and radiate more energy. These effects power the brightest objects in the universe — quasars and active galactic nuclei.
