01 — What is this data?

The SPARC (Spitzer Photometry and Accurate Rotation Curves) database contains rotation curves for 175 nearby galaxies. Each data point records the orbital velocity of gas and stars at a given distance from the galaxy center, along with the velocity predicted from visible matter alone. A total of 3,375 measurements span from galaxy cores to their outermost edges.

Stars at the edge of galaxies orbit too fast. Newtonian gravity predicts they should slow down (like planets far from the Sun). Instead they maintain constant speed. The only explanation: there's invisible mass — dark matter — holding them in orbit.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

94% flat rotation curves

94% of galaxies show flat rotation curves beyond the visible disk — velocities stay constant where Newtonian gravity predicts they should drop. The classic signature of dark matter.

2.7x mass discrepancy

Mass discrepancy ratio D = V_obs² / V_bar² grows from 0.9x at the galaxy center to 2.7x at the outer radius. The missing mass increases with distance from the core.

57% dark matter fraction

Mean dark matter fraction f_DM = 57% (median 65%). More than half of every galaxy's mass is invisible — detectable only through its gravitational pull on visible matter.

Radial Acceleration Relation

The observed acceleration correlates tightly with baryonic acceleration, with scatter of 0.196 dex. This tight relation constrains dark matter halo profiles across all galaxy types.

05 — Why this matters

This is the strongest evidence for dark matter, first discovered by Vera Rubin in the 1970s. Galaxy rotation curves should follow Keplerian decline — velocity dropping as 1/sqrt(r) beyond the visible disk, just like planets orbiting the Sun. Instead, they stay flat. The only way to explain this within known physics is a massive halo of invisible matter surrounding every galaxy.

ProtoScience found the same anomaly blindly from velocity data. Without knowing about dark matter, it detected that galaxies contain 57% invisible mass and that the discrepancy grows systematically with radius — exactly reproducing one of the most important discoveries in modern astrophysics.
