01 — What is this data?

NASA's Center for Near-Earth Object Studies (CNEOS) at JPL tracks every fireball bright enough to be detected by US government sensors. The dataset contains 1,052 fireball events with measured impact energy (in kilotons of TNT), radiated light energy, velocity, and altitude. Mass ranges from 199 kg to 10.7 million kg — pebbles to apartment buildings.

We asked ProtoScience: what fraction of a meteor's kinetic energy becomes light? And how are meteor sizes distributed?

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Luminous efficiency: 8.2%

On average, 8.2% of a meteor's kinetic energy is converted to visible light. The physics literature estimates 5-10%. ProtoScience landed right in the middle.

Power-law distribution

N(>E) ~ E^(-0.72) with R²=0.992. Fireballs follow a clean power law — lots of small ones, very few large ones. This matches asteroid population models.

Enormous mass range

199 kg to 10.7 million kg. Five orders of magnitude. The Chelyabinsk event (2013) sits near the top.

Altitude-velocity link

Correlation ρ=0.157, statistically significant. Faster meteors tend to break up at slightly higher altitudes — their kinetic energy overwhelms structural integrity sooner.

05 — Why this matters

Understanding how meteor kinetic energy converts to light is critical for planetary defense. When we detect a bright fireball, we need to estimate the actual impactor energy — and that conversion factor of 8.2% is exactly what allows that calculation. ProtoScience derived it from data alone, matching decades of calibration work by meteor physicists.

The power-law distribution tells us how frequently dangerous impacts occur: scaling from the 1,052 observed events to larger energies gives impact rates consistent with crater counting studies.
