01 — What is this data?

NASA's C-MAPSS dataset tracks 100 turbofan jet engines from healthy to failure. Each engine has 21 sensors measuring temperatures, pressures, and speeds every operating cycle. Engines lasted between 128 and 362 cycles before failing. This is real run-to-failure data used by aerospace engineers to build predictive maintenance systems.

We gave ProtoScience the raw sensor streams with no labels, no failure times, and no physics knowledge — just 20,631 rows of numbers.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Top degradation sensors

Ps30 (compressor pressure, ρ=0.71), T50 (exhaust temp, ρ=0.69), and φ (flow coefficient, ρ=-0.68) are the strongest degradation indicators.

7 sensors are useless

T2, P2, P15, epr, farB, Nf_dmd, and PCNfR_dmd carry zero information — they are constant across all engines. A third of the sensors are noise.

Nonlinear compressor physics

Ps30² dominates ALL six discovered equations. Degradation is not linear — it accelerates as compressor pressure rises. This matches known turbomachinery physics.

Every engine is unique

Individual engines fit at R²=0.30-0.40, but fleet-pooled R²=0.15. Each engine degrades along its own trajectory — there is no single universal law.

03 — Discovered equations

dPs30/dt = +0.082 − 0.239·Ps30² + 0.021·Ps30 − 0.019·P30 + 0.018·BPR Key: Ps30² term dominates — nonlinear compressor degradation Per-engine R² = 0.30–0.40 | Fleet-pooled R² = 0.15

05 — Why this matters

Predictive maintenance for jet engines is a billion-dollar problem. Current approaches use deep learning black boxes that cannot explain why an engine is failing. ProtoScience found interpretable equations that point directly to compressor pressure as the dominant failure mode — matching what aerospace engineers already know, but discovered entirely from raw data.

The finding that each engine is unique (fleet-pooled R²=0.15) explains why one-size-fits-all models underperform: effective prognostics must be personalized per engine.
