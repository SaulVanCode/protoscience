01 — What is this data?

NASA's Exoplanet Archive contains every confirmed planet outside our solar system — 3,519 worlds with measured orbital periods, distances from their star, and stellar masses. Johannes Kepler published his third law in 1619: a planet's orbital period squared equals its distance cubed (divided by the star's mass).

We gave ProtoScience the raw catalog. No hints about Kepler. No physics textbook. Just numbers.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Kepler's Third Law

P² = 0.992·a³/M☆ with slope 0.9988 (expecting 1.0) and R²=0.998172. A 400-year-old law rediscovered from scratch.

Direct power law

P ~ a^1.457 (expecting 1.5 from Kepler). R²=0.989. The exponent is within 3% of the theoretical value.

Mass-radius split

Rocky planets: R ~ M^0.42. Gas giants: R ~ M^0.05. Giant planets barely grow with mass — degeneracy pressure limits their radius.

Equilibrium temperature

T_eq = 0.992·T☆·√(R☆/2a), R²=0.968. The energy balance equation emerges from data alone.

05 — Why this matters

Kepler's Third Law is one of the most famous results in the history of science. It took Kepler years of painstaking work with Tycho Brahe's data. ProtoScience rediscovered it in seconds from a modern dataset — and got the coefficient right to three decimal places.

This is not just a party trick. It demonstrates that the system can autonomously recover fundamental physical laws from observational data, providing a benchmark for its ability to find new laws in domains where we do not yet know the answer.
