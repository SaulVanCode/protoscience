01 — What is this data?

The NASA Exoplanet Archive contains confirmed transiting planets detected by the TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite) mission. We analyzed 233 confirmed transiting planets with measured radii, orbital periods, transit depths, and host star properties.

When a planet passes in front of its star, the star dims slightly. How much it dims tells you the planet's size. TESS watches millions of stars for these tiny dips. We gave ProtoScience the transit data — no physics, no formulas, just numbers.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Geometric transit depth law

Transit depth is proportional to (R_planet / R_star)² with R² = 0.85. The planet's shadow is a simple geometric circle blocking a fraction of the stellar disk.

Duration-period scaling

Transit duration scales as P^0.37, close to the theoretical P^0.33 (cube root). Longer-period planets take longer to cross the stellar disk because they orbit at greater distances.

5 habitable zone candidates

Five planets have equilibrium temperatures between 200-350 K — the range where liquid water could exist on a rocky surface. These are the most promising targets for follow-up.

TOI-715 b at 277 K

The best candidate orbits at an equilibrium temperature of 277 K (4°C) — just above freezing. A rocky world at this temperature could host liquid water oceans.

05 — Why this matters

ProtoScience confirmed the geometric transit law and found 5 planets at temperatures where liquid water could exist. The transit method is how we find most exoplanets today — the tiny dip in brightness as a planet crosses its star is remarkably informative. From that single signal, you can measure the planet's size, orbital period, and distance from its star.
