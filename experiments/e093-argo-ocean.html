01 — What is this data?

The Argo program is a global array of over 3,000 autonomous floats that drift through the world's oceans, measuring temperature, salinity, and pressure from the surface down to 2,000 meters. It is the largest ocean observing system ever deployed.

We pulled 1,000 real profiles from five ocean basins — North Pacific, North Atlantic, South Atlantic, Indian Ocean, and South Pacific — via the Argovis API. No oceanography textbook was provided. Just raw numbers: temperature, salinity, and pressure.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Equation of State (EOS-80)

ρ = 999.84 + 0.0679·T − 0.00910·T² + 0.802·S + 4.5×10&sup6;·P. Every coefficient matches the UNESCO standard to 5+ significant figures. R²=1.000.

Temperature-Salinity coupling

T and S are strongly correlated (r=0.78) across the global ocean. This reflects water mass mixing — warm water tends to be saltier (evaporation), cold water fresher (polar runoff).

Nonlinear thermal expansion

The T² term (−0.00910) captures the nonlinear thermal expansion of seawater. Warm water expands faster per degree than cold water — critical for sea level rise projections.

Five ocean basins

Data spans the North Pacific, North Atlantic, South Atlantic, Indian, and South Pacific oceans. The same equation of state holds everywhere — a universal law of the ocean.

04 — Why it matters

The equation of state of seawater is the most fundamental equation in physical oceanography. It determines ocean currents, heat transport, and sea level. UNESCO adopted the EOS-80 standard in 1980 after decades of laboratory measurements.

ProtoScience recovered it exactly from in-situ Argo float measurements — no laboratory, no calibration tables, no textbook. The system identified that density depends on temperature (linearly and quadratically), salinity (linearly), and pressure (weakly), matching the known physics to machine precision.
