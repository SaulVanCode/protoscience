01 — What is this data?

When we observe a black hole, our viewing angle matters. The Event Horizon Telescope sees M87* nearly face-on, but Sgr A* is viewed at a steep inclination. We simulated 5 spin values crossed with 40 observer inclination angles (0° to 90°), producing 200 data points of shadow geometry, Doppler shifts, and flux ratios.

ProtoScience received the full grid of observables with no guidance about what to look for or how viewing geometry should affect the shadow.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Exact Invariant

cx · sin(θ) = constant with R²=1.000. The horizontal shadow displacement times sin(inclination) is perfectly conserved — an exact geometric invariant of the Kerr metric.

Schwarzschild Isotropy

For zero spin, 8 out of 9 observables are completely independent of viewing angle. A non-spinning black hole looks the same from every direction — perfect spherical symmetry.

Shadow Area Robustness

Even for spinning black holes, the shadow area varies by only ~1.5% across all viewing angles. The shadow is remarkably robust to observer orientation.

Doppler Scaling

The flux ratio (brightness asymmetry from approaching vs. receding sides) scales linearly with sin(θ). Edge-on observers see maximum Doppler boosting.

04 — Why it matters

The discovery that cx·sin(θ) is exactly conserved is a geometric property of the Kerr metric that is rarely highlighted in textbooks. ProtoScience found it autonomously by sweeping inclination angles — a result that connects shadow displacement to observer geometry through a clean, exact invariant.

The near-constancy of shadow area across viewing angles has practical significance: it means black hole mass measurements from shadow size are robust regardless of our uncertain knowledge of the inclination. This validates the approach used by the Event Horizon Telescope to weigh M87* and Sgr A* from their shadow diameters.
