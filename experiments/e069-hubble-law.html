01 — What is this data?

NASA's Extragalactic Distances Database (NED-D) contains distance measurements and redshifts for thousands of galaxies. We pulled 5,060 galaxies with both distance and redshift data, then filtered to 709 nearby galaxies where peculiar velocities don't dominate the Hubble flow.

Galaxies are moving away from us. The farther they are, the faster they recede. This is Hubble's Law — discovered in 1929, it proved the universe is expanding. We asked ProtoScience to find the relationship between galaxy distance and velocity. No cosmology. No hint that expansion exists. Just a table of numbers.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

H₀ = 69.7 km/s/Mpc

v = 69.7 · d — recession velocity is proportional to distance. This is the Hubble constant, landing between the Planck value (67.4) and SH0ES measurement (73.0).

R² = 0.81

The linear fit explains 81% of the variance. The remaining scatter comes from peculiar velocities — galaxies moving due to local gravitational attraction on top of cosmic expansion.

H₀ convergence

H₀ starts at 73.5 km/s/Mpc for galaxies at 10-50 Mpc, then settles to ~69 beyond 150 Mpc as peculiar velocities become negligible compared to the Hubble flow.

Peculiar velocity scatter

Residual scatter of 2,548 km/s around the fit line. This is the signature of galaxies being pulled by nearby clusters and superclusters — local gravity on top of cosmic expansion.

05 — Why this matters

Edwin Hubble needed years of careful observations and the world's largest telescope at Mount Wilson to measure the distances and velocities of a few dozen galaxies. His 1929 paper — with just 24 data points — changed our understanding of the universe forever.

ProtoScience found the same law from a table of numbers in seconds. The Hubble constant it measured (69.7 km/s/Mpc) falls squarely between the two competing modern measurements — the "Hubble tension" that is one of the biggest open problems in cosmology. The system even detected how H₀ converges with distance as peculiar velocities wash out, a subtlety that took decades of observational work to establish.
