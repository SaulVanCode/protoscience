01 — What is this data?

The World Bank collects economic, health, and demographic data from 261 countries and territories. We pulled GDP per capita, life expectancy, fertility rate, suicide rate, CO2 emissions, education spending, health spending, and population density — all via the World Bank Open Data API.

We also added latitude and approximate temperature for 119 countries. No hypotheses were provided. ProtoScience tested all variable pairs looking for mathematical relationships.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

The Preston Curve

Life expectancy = 33.7 + 10.0 · log&sub1;&sub0;(GDP). R²=0.644. Doubling GDP adds ~3 years of life — but the first $10K matters far more than the next $100K. Diminishing returns.

Demographic transition

Fertility ~ GDP−0.252. R²=0.593. Another quarter-power law in a completely different domain. Rich countries have fewer children, with an exponent close to −1/4.

Latitude and suicide

Suicide rate = 3.9 + 0.16 · |latitude|. R²=0.229. Every 10 degrees north = +1.6 suicides per 100K. The Nordic paradox is real and measurable.

What DOESN'T work

Education spending vs life expectancy: R²=0.018. Population density vs GDP: R²=0.022. GDP vs suicide: R²=0.037. Money, density, and education spending don't predict what you'd expect.

04 — Why it matters

This experiment is deliberately different from every other ProtoScience experiment. Physics has R²>0.99. Social systems peak at R²=0.66. The gap of 0.337 quantifies something profound: human societies are 51% noisier than planetary orbits.

The Preston Curve (GDP vs life expectancy) was first described by Samuel Preston in 1975. ProtoScience rediscovered it from raw World Bank data, finding that doubling GDP adds approximately 3 years of life — but with massive scatter. A country with $5K GDP can have life expectancy anywhere from 55 to 78. The equation captures the trend, not the destiny.

The fertility quarter-power (−0.252 ≈ −1/4) is an unexpected echo of biological scaling. Kleiber's Law, Gompertz mortality, and now demographic transition — all with exponents near multiples of 1/4. Whether this is coincidence or structure remains an open question.
