01 — What is this data?

The data is ProtoScience itself. Every law the system has discovered — from Kepler's orbits to Kleiber's metabolism to Gompertz's mortality — becomes a data point. Each law has an exponent, an R², a sample size, a domain, a data type, and a year of original discovery.

The question: are there patterns in the patterns? Does nature prefer certain exponents? Are some sciences inherently noisier than others? Does sample size predict how clean a law will be?

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Exponents cluster near simple fractions

Nature's power-law exponents are 0.30× as far from simple fractions (0, ¼, ½, ¾, 1, 2, 4) as random numbers would be. The universe prefers clean math.

Hierarchy of precision

GR (R²=0.999) > Astrophysics (0.971) > Physics (0.954) > Cosmology (0.930) > Biology (0.792) > Engineering (0.380). Fundamental sciences produce cleaner laws.

Simulated vs real: Δ0.104

Simulated data yields R²=1.000. Real data averages R²=0.896. The gap quantifies how much "reality" adds noise to clean mathematical laws.

Sample size doesn't matter much

Correlation between log(n) and R² is only r=0.14. Having 15,000 stars doesn't help much more than having 50 simulated black holes. The phenomenon matters more than the data.

04 — Why it matters

The exponent clustering result is the most surprising finding. If nature's laws had arbitrary exponents, we'd expect a mean distance of ~0.125 to the nearest simple fraction. The actual distance is 0.037 — three times closer than chance. This quantifies something physicists have always felt intuitively: the universe runs on simple math.

The domain hierarchy is not new — but it has never been measured this way. Each domain's mean R² is a quantitative "noise floor" — the inherent messiness of that field's phenomena. Biology is noisier than physics because organisms have more degrees of freedom than planets.

This experiment turns ProtoScience inward. The tool that discovers laws has discovered a pattern in its own discoveries. That's either recursive genius or a sign to stop and write the paper.
