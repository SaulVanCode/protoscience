01 — What is this data?

The Royal Observatory of Belgium (SILSO) has maintained a continuous record of monthly sunspot numbers since 1749. This is one of the longest continuous scientific datasets in existence — 277 years and 3,326 monthly observations. Sunspot counts range from zero (during solar minimum) to over 250 (during the most active peaks).

We gave ProtoScience the raw monthly numbers. No mention of "solar cycles" or any periodicity hints. Just a column of integers spanning three centuries.

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Schwabe cycle: 11.09 years

FFT peak at 11.09 years, matching the known 11-year solar cycle discovered by Schwabe in 1843. ProtoScience's error: just 0.09 years (about 1 month).

Gleissberg modulation: 93 years

A second FFT peak at 92.39 years — the Gleissberg cycle, a slow modulation of solar activity amplitude. This was only recognized by solar physicists in the 1950s.

Waldmeier effect

Rise time ~0.6 years, decline ~8.3 years. Solar cycles ramp up fast and decay slowly — an asymmetry first described by Waldmeier in 1935.

30 individual cycles

Peak detection identified all 30 solar cycles in the record, each with its own amplitude and duration. No two cycles are identical.

04 — Visualization

Monthly sunspot numbers from 1749 to 2026. Each peak is one solar cycle. Notice how peak amplitudes rise and fall over ~93-year timescales (the Gleissberg modulation).

05 — Why this matters

The sunspot cycle drives space weather — solar flares, coronal mass ejections, and geomagnetic storms that can knock out power grids and satellites. Understanding its periodicity is critical for infrastructure planning. ProtoScience recovered both the primary 11-year cycle and the 93-year amplitude modulation that determines whether a given cycle will be strong or weak.

Finding the Waldmeier asymmetry (fast rise, slow decline) without any physics knowledge demonstrates that ProtoScience captures waveform shape, not just frequency — a much harder problem than simple periodicity detection.
