01 — What is this data?

In August 2012, Voyager 1 crossed the heliopause — the boundary where the Sun's magnetic influence ends and interstellar space begins. NASA's magnetometer recorded the magnetic field every 48 seconds, producing 156,909 measurements throughout the year. This is the only dataset ever recorded of a spacecraft leaving the solar system.

We gave ProtoScience the raw magnetic field vectors. No dates. No labels. No hint that a boundary existed. Could it find the heliopause blind?

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

Magnetic field doubles

|B| jumps from 0.279 nT to 0.562 nT at the crossing — a clean factor of 2. The interstellar magnetic field is stronger than the heliosheath.

Chaos to order

Before the crossing, the field is chaotic (SINDy R²=0.12 — basically random). After the crossing, it becomes highly ordered (R²=0.97). The Sun's turbulence gives way to galactic calm.

Statistically certain

Mann-Whitney p = 3.3 × 10⁻²⁰. The probability that this is random chance is essentially zero — 20 orders of magnitude below any significance threshold.

Higher variability inside

Variability is 1.2x higher in the heliosheath. The Sun's magnetic field is turbulent and fluctuating; interstellar space is smooth.

05 — Why this matters

The heliopause crossing was one of the most significant events in space exploration history. NASA scientists spent months debating whether Voyager had actually crossed. ProtoScience found the boundary automatically — and quantified the transition with statistical certainty that leaves no room for doubt.

More broadly, this demonstrates that ProtoScience can detect regime changes in time-series data without prior knowledge. The same capability applies to detecting phase transitions in materials, market regime changes in finance, or climate tipping points in Earth science.
