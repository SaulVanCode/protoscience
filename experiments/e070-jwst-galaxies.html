01 — What is this data?

The James Webb Space Telescope can see galaxies so far away that their light has been traveling for billions of years. Higher redshift means further back in time. z>10 means we're seeing galaxies when the universe was only 400 million years old.

The JWST UNCOVER DR3 catalog covers the Abell 2744 galaxy cluster field — one of the deepest views ever taken of the universe. It contains 74,020 galaxies, of which 62,572 have valid photometric redshifts and size measurements. We gave ProtoScience the full catalog with no instructions beyond "find the patterns."

02 — What did ProtoScience discover?

4,589 galaxies at z>6

The reionization era — when the first stars and galaxies ionized the neutral hydrogen that filled the early universe. JWST found thousands of galaxies in this critical epoch.

1,042 galaxies at z>10

The first 400 million years after the Big Bang. These are among the earliest galaxies ever detected — objects that existed when the universe was less than 3% of its current age.

Size evolution: (1+z)^(-0.17)

Galaxies were physically smaller in the early universe. Effective radius scales as r ~ (1+z)^(-0.17), meaning galaxies at z=10 are roughly half the size of similar galaxies today.

Photo-z calibration: 0.041

σ_NMAD = 0.041 — the photometric redshift scatter. This means distance estimates are accurate to about 4%, enabling reliable population statistics across cosmic time.

05 — Why this matters

The James Webb Space Telescope is the most expensive scientific instrument ever built ($10B). This catalog captures its view of 74,000 galaxies across 13 billion years of cosmic history — from the present day all the way back to the first few hundred million years after the Big Bang.

Before JWST, we had detected fewer than a dozen galaxies beyond z=10. This single field contains over a thousand. ProtoScience independently found the size-evolution power law and identified the reionization-era population — the same results that took teams of astronomers months to publish.
